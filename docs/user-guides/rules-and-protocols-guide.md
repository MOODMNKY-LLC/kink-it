## What Are Rules & Protocols?

**Rules** are specific expectations and guidelines that govern behavior in your dynamic. They can be:
- **Standing Rules**: Permanent guidelines (e.g., honorific usage, transparency default)
- **Situational Rules**: Context-specific expectations (e.g., permission systems, meal reporting)
- **Temporary Rules**: Time-limited expectations (e.g., weekly reflection task)
- **Protocols**: Ritualized behaviors that reinforce your dynamic (e.g., morning coffee service, evening kneeling)

**Protocols** are structured rituals that create predictable patterns and reinforce power exchange. They transform ordinary activities into meaningful expressions of your dynamic.

## Creating Rules & Protocols

### For Dominants

#### Step 1: Identify What Needs Structure

Think about areas where structure would benefit your dynamic:
- Daily routines (morning, evening)
- Communication patterns
- Service expectations
- Permission systems
- Accountability measures

#### Step 2: Define the Rule Clearly

A good rule includes:
- **Title**: Clear, concise name
- **Description**: Detailed explanation of what's expected
- **Category**: Standing, situational, temporary, or protocol
- **Acceptance Criteria**: How you'll know it's completed correctly
- **Why It Matters**: The purpose and benefit

**Example Structure:**
```
Title: Morning Greeting Protocol
Description: Each morning, you will send a "Good morning, Sir" message before 8:00 AM. Include a brief note about your energy level and any concerns for the day.
Category: Protocol
Acceptance Criteria: Message sent before 8:00 AM with greeting, energy level, and any concerns
Why It Matters: Maintains connection and allows me to adjust expectations based on your state
```

#### Step 3: Set Priority and Status

- **Priority**: Higher numbers = more important (1-10 scale)
- **Status**: Active, Inactive, or Archived
- **Effective Dates**: When the rule starts/ends (for temporary rules)

#### Step 4: Add Metadata (Optional)

Use the metadata field to store:
- Step-by-step instructions
- Linked tasks
- Exceptions or special circumstances
- Review dates

#### Step 5: Communicate and Implement

1. Create the rule in the app
2. Discuss it with your submissive
3. Allow questions and clarification
4. Start implementation
5. Review and adjust as needed

### For Submissives

#### Understanding Rules

When your Dominant creates a rule:
1. **Read Carefully**: Understand exactly what's expected
2. **Ask Questions**: Clarify anything unclear
3. **Note Acceptance Criteria**: Know how to complete it correctly
4. **Understand Purpose**: Know why it matters
5. **Commit**: Agree to follow the rule

#### Following Protocols

Protocols are ritualized behaviors. When following a protocol:
1. **Follow Steps Precisely**: Protocols have specific steps for a reason
2. **Maintain Presence**: Be mindful, not robotic
3. **Communicate Issues**: If something isn't working, say so
4. **Practice**: Some protocols improve with repetition

## Best Practices

### For Dominants

1. **Start Small**: Begin with 2-3 rules, add more gradually
2. **Be Clear**: Ambiguity creates anxiety - be specific
3. **Explain Purpose**: Help your submissive understand why
4. **Review Regularly**: Check what's working, adjust what isn't
5. **Be Flexible**: Life happens - adapt when needed
6. **Acknowledge Effort**: Recognize when protocols are followed well

### For Submissives

1. **Ask Questions**: Clarify expectations before committing
2. **Be Honest**: If something isn't working, communicate
3. **Practice Presence**: Protocols aren't checklists - be mindful
4. **Accept Imperfection**: You'll make mistakes - that's okay
5. **Request Adjustments**: If a rule needs modification, ask

### Protocol Design Principles

1. **Meaningful**: Should reinforce your dynamic, not just create busywork
2. **Sustainable**: Must be maintainable long-term
3. **Adaptable**: Should adjust when life circumstances change
4. **Consistent**: Regular practice reinforces the dynamic
5. **Respectful**: Should support both partners' wellbeing

## Managing Rules

### Editing Rules

- **Dominants**: Can edit any rule they created
- **Submissives**: Can view all rules but cannot edit
- **Status Changes**: Rules can be activated, deactivated, or archived

### Rule Categories Explained

**Standing Rules**: Permanent guidelines that form the foundation of your dynamic. Examples: honorific usage, transparency expectations, communication standards.

**Situational Rules**: Context-specific expectations that apply in certain situations. Examples: permission systems, meal reporting, activity requests.

**Temporary Rules**: Time-limited expectations with specific end dates. Examples: weekly reflection tasks, short-term training protocols, trial periods.

**Protocols**: Ritualized behaviors with specific steps and purposes. Examples: morning coffee service, evening kneeling, greeting rituals.

## Troubleshooting

### "Rules Feel Like a Checklist"

**Problem**: Protocols feel robotic rather than meaningful.

**Solution**: 
- Review the "why it matters" for each rule
- Add mindfulness elements (reflection, presence)
- Reduce number of rules if overwhelming
- Focus on quality over quantity

### "Too Many Rules"

**Problem**: Overwhelmed by number of rules.

**Solution**:
- Archive or deactivate less critical rules
- Combine similar rules
- Prioritize what matters most
- Start fresh with fewer, more meaningful rules

### "Rules Aren't Being Followed"

**Problem**: Submissive struggling to follow rules consistently.

**Solution**:
- Check if rules are clear and achievable
- Discuss what's challenging
- Adjust expectations if needed
- Consider if rules align with submissive's capacity
